1: Chiller Plant and Seawater Pump House

HKUST's campus location provides an excellent opportunity to use a seawater cooling system: an inexpensive and energy-efficient cooling system for centralized air conditioning plants. Compared to an air cooling system, seawater cooling systems save substantial amounts of energy. Water discharge is regularly monitored to assure compliance with environmental regulatory requirements. The large concrete block found about 200 metres offshore marks the seawater intake.

2: LED Outdoor Lighting Pilot Program

The LED lights found on the seafront are a pilot project of the Climate Group’s “LightSavers” program, which aims to transform how the world is lit by demonstrating the potential ways in which LED lighting and smart controls can curb greenhouse gas emissions and cut energy costs. The program also aims to promote LED lighting on a global scale, aiming to make cities brighter and safer at night. LED lights are now used to light our piazza and the “Red Bird” sundial. Since December 2007, HKUST has adopted the HKBEAM (Building Environmental Assessment Method) system’s Gold Standard as the minimum performance level to be achieved for new buildings on campus. Find out more about the Gold Standard here: https://www. ab.ust.hk/fmo/Projects/Sustainability-Policy.htm

4: Piazza and Academic Buildings

The façade in the Piazza provides shading and reduces solar gain in the afternoon when the sun is in the southwest. The offices and classrooms in the academic building were designed with many windows and glass curtain walls facing east, reducing solar exposure. The glass panes in the Academic Concourse allow natural light in the morning when the building is cool, while recent extensions provide shading. Tinted and reflective coatings help reduce solar gain through windows.

5: Lee Shau Kee Business Building, Institute for Advanced Study

Completed in 2013, the Lee Shau Kee Business Building and the Institute for Advanced Study have a number of sustainable features, including water-cooled chillers, heat recovery fresh air pre-conditioning, occupancy and daylight sensors for lighting control, automatic lighting and ventilation control for lifts, solar lighting in landscaped areas and a cooling water tower for flushing. These are the first double glazed buildings on campus. Solar gain is further minimized by the use of a low emissivity coating on the glass.

6: Student Halls VIII and IX

The new Student Halls VIII and IX opened in 2012, providing 700 places for student residents. These buildings have a number of green features, including 29 solar water heater panels to preheat incoming city water upstream of the domestic hot water system, a roof garden to reduce solar heat load, occupancy and daylight sensors for lighting control, automatic lighting and ventilation control for lifts, solar lighting in landscaped areas and electricity meters on each floor.

7: Tree Preservation for New Campus Development

To reduce the environmental impact of new building construction on campus, surveys are conducted and plans for tree felling, transplantation and compensatory planting are agreed with government before any work starts. The relevant Tree Preservation ordinance states that compensatory planting should be made on site, in at least a 1:1 ratio in terms of both quality and quantity. During the construction of the Lee Shau Kee Campus and Student Halls VII and IX, some of the removed trees were either transplanted or compensated for around the seafront, LG7 Lawn, Lee Shau Kee Campus and Coastal Marine Lab areas. To find out more, click here: https://www.ab.ust.hk/fmo/ Projects/Landscaping-Development.htm

8: The Costal Marine Laboratory

Established in 2002, HKUST’s Coastal Marine Laboratory provides cutting-edge facilities for marine science research in Hong Kong and south China. Its location at Port Shelter provides direct access to highquality seawater and close proximity to a variety of marine habitats. The large-scale aquarium supports a broad diversity of research activities in area of marine ecology, marine pollution and environmental chemistry. Click here to discover more: http://www.ust.hk/cml/ introduction.html

9: The HKUST Air Quality Research Supersite

This facility is home to the first Hong Kong Air Quality Supersite Study, focusing on the real-time characterization of ambient particulate matter. Gas and particle instruments began taking measurements in 2011. The site enhances air quality research and is helping to tackle air pollution problems in Hong Kong and the Pearl River Delta, including a number of air quality monitoring projects conducted in collaboration with Hong Kong’s Environmental Protection Department. http://www.envr.ust.hk/supersite/

11: Campus Caterers’ Food Waste Recycling Facilities

HKUST has committed to reducing and recycling food waste since 2003. To support the government’s “Food Wise Hong Kong” campaign launched in 2013, the University will further expand this program to promote food waste reduction among students and staff. New food waste composting machines will be installed at different catering outlets, which will turn food waste into soil conditioner and fertilizer for campus use. Other ongoing green initiatives include the “less rice” promotion, offering more vegetarian dishes, and providing a discount for bringing your own lunch box and coffee mug.

12: Seafront “Glassphalt” Road

This road is a “demo” of a waste glass bottle research project conducted by the Department of Chemical Engineering and the Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering. Glass cullet made from used drink bottles was used instead of fine and coarse aggregates. The new material, called “Glassphalt”, was proven to perform as well as ordinary asphalt in road paving.

13: Laboratory for Environmental Compliance and Monitoring

Our operations comply with all applicable environmental regulatory requirements, including monitoring of wastewater, seawater discharge, hazardous waste management, air quality and environmental noise level. A chemical exchange program encourages users to donate unwanted chemicals as free “giveaways”, to eligible request-makers on campus and other universities.
